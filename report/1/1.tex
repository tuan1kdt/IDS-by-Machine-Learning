\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=======================================================================

The global cyber-threat landscape has grown sharply in both volume and sophistication over the last decade. Enterprises routinely face volumetric Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attacks, SSH and FTP brute-force credential-stuffing, web-application injection, internal reconnaissance, and malware command-and-control (C2) beaconing--- often simultaneously and across heterogeneous network segments. An \textbf{Intrusion Detection System (IDS)} is therefore a foundational layer of defense-in-depth, continuously monitoring traffic and raising alerts when it observes activity that deviates from expected patterns~\cite{ref_anomaly_survey}.

Traditional IDSs fall into two broad families: \emph{signature-based} detectors that match traffic against a curated rule database (e.g., Snort, Suricata), and \emph{anomaly-based} detectors that model normal behavior and flag statistical deviations. Signature approaches are precise but brittle---they require human-authored rules and cannot detect \emph{zero-day} attacks or polymorphic malware whose signatures drift. Anomaly approaches generalize better but have historically suffered from high false-positive rates that overwhelm analysts~\cite{ref_dl_ids}. These limitations motivate a third generation of detectors: \textbf{machine-learning-based IDSs} that learn discriminative traffic representations directly from labeled data.

In this project we design, implement, and evaluate an AI-powered IDS that classifies network flows in near real time. Concretely, we make the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{An end-to-end pipeline} from raw packet captures (PCAPs) to per-flow classification, comprising: feature extraction (CICFlowMeter-style statistics), pre-processing and class-imbalance handling via \textbf{SMOTE}~\cite{ref_smote}, training of three candidate models, and a streaming inference engine exposed over a simple REST/WebSocket interface.
    \item \textbf{A comparative study} of three learners---Random Forest (RF)~\cite{ref_rf}, XGBoost~\cite{ref_xgboost}, and a compact feed-forward Deep Neural Network (DNN) implemented in TensorFlow~\cite{ref_tensorflow}---on the widely-adopted \textbf{CIC-IDS2017}~\cite{ref_cicids2017} benchmark, covering DDoS, PortScan, Brute-Force, Web Attack, Infiltration, Bot, and benign traffic classes.
    \item \textbf{A live demonstration} in a controlled lab environment. We generate synthetic DDoS, SSH brute-force, and malware-like C2 traffic alongside benign background traffic and measure end-to-end detection accuracy, false-positive rate (FPR), and per-flow inference latency.
    \item \textbf{A deployment discussion} covering threat-model assumptions, concept drift, adversarial robustness, and pathways to online/continual learning.
\end{enumerate}

Our headline result is that a Random Forest trained on CICFlowMeter features achieves a macro-$F_1$ score of $0.996$ on the held-out CIC-IDS2017 test split while sustaining sub-millisecond inference per flow on commodity CPU. A compact DNN matches this accuracy within $0.5$ percentage points at roughly three times lower inference cost, making it the preferred choice for line-rate deployment.

Section~\ref{sec:dataset} describes the CIC-IDS2017 dataset, feature set, and pre-processing pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:models} details the three candidate models and their training protocols. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes and outlines future work.
