\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%=======================================================================

The global cyber-threat landscape has grown sharply in both volume and sophistication over the last decade. Enterprises routinely face volumetric Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attacks, SSH and FTP brute-force credential-stuffing, web-application injection, internal reconnaissance, and malware command-and-control (C2) beaconing---often simultaneously and across heterogeneous network segments. An \textbf{Intrusion Detection System (IDS)} is therefore a foundational layer of defense-in-depth, continuously monitoring traffic and raising alerts when it observes activity that deviates from expected patterns~\cite{ref_anomaly_survey}.

Traditional IDSs fall into two broad families: \emph{signature-based} detectors that match traffic against a curated rule database (e.g., Snort, Suricata), and \emph{anomaly-based} detectors that model normal behavior and flag statistical deviations. Signature approaches are precise but brittle---they require human-authored rules and cannot detect zero-day attacks or polymorphic malware whose signatures drift. Anomaly approaches generalise better but have historically suffered from high false-positive rates that overwhelm analysts~\cite{ref_dl_ids}. These limitations motivate \textbf{machine-learning-based IDSs} that learn discriminative traffic representations directly from labelled data.

In this project we design, implement, and evaluate an AI-powered IDS that classifies network flows across 13 traffic classes. Concretely, we make the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{An end-to-end ML pipeline} from raw CICFlowMeter features to per-flow classification, comprising stratified sampling, Proportional SMOTE oversampling, and comparative training of four classifiers on the \textbf{CIC-IDS2017}~\cite{ref_cicids2017} benchmark.
    \item \textbf{A comparative study} of four learners---LightGBM~\cite{ref_lightgbm}, XGBoost~\cite{ref_xgboost}, Random Forest~\cite{ref_rf}, and Decision Tree---covering DDoS, PortScan, Brute-Force, DoS, Web Attack, Bot, and benign traffic classes.
    \item \textbf{An ablation study} isolating the contribution of Proportional SMOTE and identifying the systematic failure modes (Web Attack confusion, Rare\_Attack reliability) inherent to flow-level classification.
    \item \textbf{A deployment discussion} covering concept drift, adversarial robustness, and pathways to online/continual learning.
\end{enumerate}

Our headline result is that \textbf{LightGBM with Proportional SMOTE} achieves a Macro-$F_1$ of $0.9241$ on the held-out test set with six of thirteen classes reaching perfect $F_1 = 1.00$, while sustaining $> 30{,}000$ flows per second on commodity CPU hardware.

The remainder of this report is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} reviews the IDS problem formulation, the CIC-IDS2017 dataset, and the machine learning foundations. Section~\ref{sec:models} describes the end-to-end ML pipeline and the four candidate models. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} presents quantitative results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses limitations and future directions. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.
