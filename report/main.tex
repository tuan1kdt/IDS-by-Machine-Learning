\begin{abstract}
Modern enterprise networks face an ever-expanding attack surface, with adversaries continuously evolving techniques ranging from volumetric Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) floods to stealthy credential brute-force and malware command-and-control (C2) traffic. Signature-based Intrusion Detection Systems (IDSs) remain effective against known threats but fail to generalize to zero-day and polymorphic attacks. This paper presents an AI-powered IDS that applies supervised machine learning to classify network flows in near real time. We train and compare three models---Random Forest, Gradient-Boosted Trees (XGBoost), and a feed-forward Deep Neural Network (DNN) implemented in TensorFlow---on the CIC-IDS2017 benchmark dataset. We describe the end-to-end pipeline: flow-level feature extraction from raw PCAPs, class-imbalance mitigation via SMOTE, model training and hyperparameter tuning, and a streaming inference engine that evaluates flows with sub-millisecond latency per flow. We further validate the system against simulated attack traffic generated in a controlled lab (DDoS, SSH brute-force, and malware-like C2 beaconing) and report per-class detection accuracy, false-positive rate, and end-to-end latency. Experimental results show that the Random Forest model achieves a macro-$F_1$ of 0.996 while a compact DNN offers comparable accuracy at 3$\times$ lower inference cost, confirming that ML-based IDSs can generalize to unseen attack variants while meeting the latency budget required for inline deployment. We also discuss deployment considerations, adversarial robustness, and avenues for online/continual learning.

\end{abstract}
