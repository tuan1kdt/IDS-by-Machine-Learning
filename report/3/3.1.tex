\subsection{Dataset: CIC-IDS 2017}
\label{sec:dataset}

We use the \textbf{CIC-IDS 2017} dataset~\cite{ref_cicids2017}, created by the Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity at the University of New Brunswick. This dataset was generated by simulating realistic network traffic over five days (Monday--Friday), with benign background traffic and various attack scenarios executed on specific days.

\subsubsection{Dataset Characteristics.}

The dataset consists of network flows extracted using CICFlowMeter, with \textbf{78 network flow features} including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Flow-level features}: Flow duration, total forward/backward packets, total forward/backward bytes.
    \item \textbf{Statistical features}: Mean, standard deviation, max, min of packet lengths and inter-arrival times.
    \item \textbf{Flag-based features}: Counts of SYN, FIN, RST, PSH, ACK, URG, CWE, ECE flags.
    \item \textbf{Flow characteristics}: Bytes/second, packets/second, average packet size, subflow metrics.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Advantages Over KDD Cup 99.}

The KDD Cup 99 dataset~\cite{ref_kdd99}, while historically important, suffers from well-documented issues~\cite{ref_nsl_kdd}: (1) highly redundant records that bias classifiers toward frequent patterns, (2) unrealistic traffic distribution, and (3) outdated attack types from 1998. CIC-IDS 2017 addresses these limitations with modern attack scenarios, realistic traffic profiles generated from user behavior abstractions, and comprehensive flow-based feature extraction.
